Only with  the ownership of the means  of production by African-American entrepreneurs, first  attempted by  Sam  Cooke,  and  then  with  more  sustained efforts by those  like Berry Gordy of Motown, music  producers like Quincy Jones, and  extremely wealthy performers like Michael  Jackson, who  were  eventually to develop the economic clout  capable of buying the catalogues of white  musicians like the Beatles as well as those  of African-American performers, did  the cycle of appropriation,  exploitation and  disenfranchisement come,  at  least  to  a  certain extent,  to an end.

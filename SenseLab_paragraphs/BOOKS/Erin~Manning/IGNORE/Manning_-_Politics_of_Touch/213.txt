Biopolitics speak of a body incorporeal, where power is already affecting the body before it has become a body, and the body is already affecting politics before politics has been deﬁned as such. Yet, skin is both corporeal and incorporeal. The body of skin is an exten- sion, always, of the body as it is not yet. Skin reminds us that the body moves all the time, composing and decomposing itself, sensing all the while. Senses play on and beyond our surfaces — our skins — enabling not only new ways of coming to experience, but different ways of thinking the relation between making sense and sensing, between knowing and sensing, between expression and content, between surface and depth.3

If we understand the virtual as a realm where differential relations coexist, we can begin to explore the potential of our bodies as generative, always- already technological (that is, prosthetic) sensory expressions. As Andrew Murphie writes: “The virtual, as a necessary part of the object, is... absolutely real, but this is in the sense of a reality which is different/ciated and pro- duced as different/ciated” (2002, 199).

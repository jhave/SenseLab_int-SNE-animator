This excess is felt as a palpable enthusiasm carrying a force of induction, a contagious involvement. Étienne Souriau remarks upon the “enthusiasm of the body” with which an animal abandons itself to the lived abstraction of play (1965, 35).7 The animal in play is intensely animated. Its vital gestures embody a heightened vivacity. They express what Daniel Stern would call a vitality affect (1985, 53–61). Enthusiasm of the body is the vitality affect of play, made palpable. The vitality affect of play, and the enthusiasm of the body it expresses, coincide with the -esque in “combatesque.”
